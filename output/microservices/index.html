Microservices

An architectural style that structures an application as a collection of loosely coupled services.

Description

In the context of Non-Human Identities, microservices refer to a design approach where software applications are composed of small, independent services that can be deployed and scaled independently. Each microservice is responsible for a specific business capability and can be developed, deployed, and maintained by different teams. This approach enables greater flexibility, scalability, and resilience in application development and management. Non-Human Identities, such as APIs, IoT devices, or digital agents, often utilize microservices to interact and process data efficiently. By adopting a microservices architecture, organizations can leverage automation, continuous integration, and delivery practices, allowing for faster updates and better resource utilization. This modular nature also facilitates easier integration with third-party services and systems, enhancing the overall functionality and interoperability of non-human entities within an ecosystem. As a result, microservices play a crucial role in enabling complex systems to operate more effectively in a world increasingly dominated by automated and digital interactions.

Examples

  • A payment processing service that operates independently from the main e-commerce application.
  • An authentication service that manages user identities and access controls across multiple applications.

Additional Information

  • Microservices can be built using different programming languages and technologies, allowing teams to choose the best tools for their needs.
  • They promote the use of DevOps practices, enabling faster releases and improved collaboration between development and operations teams.
