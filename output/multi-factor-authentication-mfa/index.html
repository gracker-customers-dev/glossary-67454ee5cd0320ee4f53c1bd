Multi-factor authentication (MFA)

A security process that requires multiple forms of verification to authenticate non-human identities.

Description

Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is a security measure that enhances the protection of non-human identities, such as applications, services, and devices, by requiring multiple forms of verification before granting access. Unlike traditional single-factor authentication methods that rely solely on passwords, MFA incorporates at least two or more independent credentials from different categories: something you know (like a password), something you have (like a security token or a mobile device), and something you are (like biometric verification). In the context of non-human identities, MFA is crucial for ensuring that automated processes, APIs, and machine-to-machine communications are secure and that only authorized entities can access sensitive data or perform critical functions. This approach reduces the risk of unauthorized access and enhances overall security posture, especially in environments where automated scripts or services operate without direct human intervention. As threats to digital identities continue to evolve, implementing MFA for non-human identities is becoming increasingly important for organizations aiming to safeguard their systems and data.

Examples

  • An API that requires a token and an IP address verification for access.
  • A cloud service that uses OAuth tokens in combination with a cryptographic key.

Additional Information

  • MFA can significantly reduce the risk of identity theft and unauthorized access.
  • Implementing MFA may require additional resources and user education to ensure compliance.
